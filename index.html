The Price of 50 Rupees

One evening I was on my way to buy tikka when an elderly woman stopped me. “Son, can you count this money?” she asked. It was 100 rupees. The tikka cost 150. She works as a street sweeper. I see her every morning cleaning roads while the city sleeps.

For us, spending money carelessly is normal. We waste what our parents earn without thinking about the sweat behind it. But have we ever asked how much effort it takes? Have we ever wondered about their dreams — the ones they quietly buried for us? The real poverty is not in the pocket. It begins when we stop valuing sacrifice.

Ego: The Small Thing That Destroys Big Things

It’s rarely something big. It’s always ego. A small disagreement turns into silence. Silence turns into distance. And distance ends friendships.

Recently, I argued with a friend and walked away, convincing myself it was about self-respect. Two days later, we were laughing again like nothing happened. That made me realize — if either of us had chosen ego over understanding, we would have lost something real over nothing.

Not every disagreement deserves the destruction of a bond. Don’t test your friends. Don’t let ego decide your relationships.

Who Is Truly Poor?

There are two kinds of poor people in this world. The first are those who lack money, food, or shelter. That poverty is visible.

The second are those who have everything, yet spend nothing on themselves or others. They hoard wealth but live with small hearts. That poverty is hidden.

In my opinion, the worst poverty is poverty of the heart. If your heart becomes poor, money cannot fix you. The real problem is never money — it is what we let money do to our hearts.

Effort or Fate?

Driving through F-8, I saw beautiful houses and boys my age driving luxury cars. A thought crossed my mind — how did they get all this? Is it business? Is it luck?

Then I saw a truck pass by with the words: “Everyone has their own destiny.” Maybe it’s true. Maybe some are tested with abundance, and others are tested with absence.

But the question remains: Is life built by effort — or written by fate? Perhaps both.

Loving After Loss

I grew up longing to be loved in a way that felt safe and complete. My grandmother gave me that love. After her, it felt like something inside me stayed empty.

I once gave everything in love and lost myself in the process. When she left, I wasn’t sure I could ever feel that way again. Then someone new entered my life. She reminded me what love can look like — soft, caring, alive.

Loving someone after heartbreak is terrifying. You give your heart knowing it might not survive another fall. But maybe love isn’t about guarantees. Maybe it’s about courage.

The First Morning After

There is death — and then there is the first morning after letting someone go.

You wake up and instinctively check your phone. No message. No call. That’s when the reality hits. The absence becomes physical. Heavy.

The world continues. But inside, something has ended.

Dreaming of a Simple Life

Sometimes I imagine a small house. A quiet café filled with the smell of chai and coffee. Two people building something together. Not rich — just enough.

A peaceful life. A small family. No noise. Just love and effort.

Maybe happiness isn’t luxury. Maybe it’s simplicity shared with the right person.

I Will Never Be This Young Again

I’m getting older every day. Dreams are beautiful — but responsibilities grow faster. One finishes, another begins.

I used to think money meant nothing. Now I understand its weight. Not everyone gets everything they want. That’s a truth our generation struggles to accept.

But sometimes, you must live a little for yourself. Buy something you like. Go somewhere peaceful. Be slightly selfish — so you don’t die full of regret.

At the end of the day, we are all temporary. The question is — did we truly live?
